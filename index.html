Current Research

Course-Based Undergraduate Research Experiences (CUREs)

Background

Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics (STEM) students have historically learned technical skills through instructional laboratory courses. In many cases, these courses ask the students to complete pre-prescribed protocols, where the instructor and students know the expected outcome. The level of inquiry in these recipe "cookbook" style labs is relatively low. Additionally, if a student does not get the expected result, they do not typically troubleshoot the issue and try again. Cookbook style experiments do not fully prepare students to work with real world problems that occur in STEM careers.

What is a CURE?

CUREs have emerged in laboratory instruction to introduce high level of inquiry and build troubleshooting and critical thinking skills while also mimicking real-world laboratory workplaces. CUREs bring an authentic research experience into the course. They have also been shown to improve students’ sense of belonging, understand what research really is like, and take ownership over their work. There are five components that make up a CURE: scientific practices, relevance outside the classroom, discovery, iteration, and collaboration. Despite the many benefits and interest in CUREs, it has not been explicitly shown that they develop better laboratory skills than a traditional course.

Curriculum

I have built a CURE-based curriculum for General Chemistry II laboratory based on the metal adsorption capacity of biochar, a material made from the pyrolysis of biomass. The first half of the semester teaches the students research skills and laboratory techniques to be able to do the CURE in the second half of the semester. The students learn how to find research articles, read the articles they find, develop a research question and hypothesis, create an experimental proposal to execute during the CURE, and how to present their work both in written and oral formats. At the end of the CURE the students write a report and do a poster presentation about their progress throughout the CURE. Our outside stakeholders attend the poster session to talk with the students about their work.

Our three research questions:

  1. Does the CURE develop the students’ experimental design skills more than a traditional lab?
    • Experimental Design Ability Test
  2. Does the CURE develop the students’ laboratory technique more than a traditional lab?
    • Digital Badging
  3. Is there a Dunning Kruger Effect in the student's laboratory skills? If so, is this lessened by the CURE curriculum?
    • Comparing self-assessments to actual scores

Gamification / Game-Based Learning

I have been collaborating to create a gamified General Chemistry II lecture course. The overal course is fantasy themed with fantasy named levels with digital badges. The grading scheme is a hybrid specifications-based and "experience" points-based. There are training materials, main quests, and side quests which students can complete for experience points that are needed to level up. In order to acheive the letter grades A-D, the students have to show proficiency in exam questions. I have built 5 ditial escape rooms that are used for exam review. The escape rooms are hosted on Google Sites.
